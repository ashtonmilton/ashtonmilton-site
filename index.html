Dissertation Plan

Selective State Capacity under Accelerated Development

Investment prioritisation, labour informalisation, and transitional inequality in Indonesia.

Indonesia Inequality State Capacity Development

Project Overview

This project examines how development states may strengthen investment-facing institutions while leaving labour, welfare, and redistributive institutions comparatively weak. Using Indonesia as a case study, the dissertation explores the relationship between investment prioritisation, labour informalisation, and transitional inequality.


The project is organised around the concept of selective state capacity: the uneven development of state capability across different institutional and policy domains. Rather than treating state capacity as a single national attribute, the project asks why some capacities develop earlier, faster, or more coherently than others.

Research Question

How does accelerated development produce uneven state capacity across investment, labour, welfare, and redistributive institutions?

Working Argument

Investment-oriented governance may strengthen because it directly supports growth and state legitimacy, while redistributive institutions remain politically and administratively harder to consolidate.

Methodological Direction

The project uses qualitative policy analysis, institutional analysis, and a case-study approach, supported by secondary data and policy literature.

Research Interests

These interests are connected by a broader concern with how development is governed, how capacity is built, and how inequality is produced or managed across the Global South.

State Capacity & Capacity Development

I am interested in how states build administrative, fiscal, welfare, and regulatory capacity over time. My work focuses less on whether a state is simply “strong” or “weak”, and more on how capacity develops unevenly across different institutions and policy areas.

Development Governance

I examine how governments manage economic transformation, infrastructure development, investment policy, labour regulation, and social provision under conditions of rapid growth and institutional constraint.

South–South Cooperation & Comparison

I am interested in how countries across Asia, Africa, Latin America, and the wider Global South compare, cooperate, and learn from one another’s development experiences. This includes policy transfer, development finance, infrastructure cooperation, and non-Western models of governance.

Inequality, Redistribution & Social Protection

A central concern in my work is how development can generate growth while leaving social protection, redistribution, labour rights, and welfare systems underdeveloped. I am interested in how inequality becomes institutionally reproduced.

Labour Informality & Precarity

I focus on the persistence of informal and precarious labour in high-growth economies. I am especially interested in how investment-led development models can weaken, bypass, or delay labour-protective institutions.

Political Economy of Southeast Asia

Southeast Asia is a core regional focus because it offers cases of accelerated but uneven development. Indonesia is my primary case study, with wider interests in ASEAN, middle-income development, and comparative Global South trajectories.

Global Governance & International Development

I am interested in how international organisations, regional institutions, development banks, and multilateral frameworks interact with domestic development priorities, especially across Asia and the Global South.

East Asian Institutional Systems

Living and studying in Japan has strengthened my interest in East Asian urban governance, public infrastructure, state coordination, and the everyday visibility of institutional capacity.
